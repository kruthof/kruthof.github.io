I study how AI systems reshape `information processing`, `decision-making`, and the `production of knowledge`.

My work examines how complex information is interpreted, incorporated into decisions, and translated into outcomes, especially in settings where AI systems mediate what people read, measure, revise, and believe. Empirically, I focus on economic, financial, scientific, and media text. Methodologically, I combine machine learning, text-based empirical analysis, benchmark design, and economic modeling.

Two questions guide my research:

> **How does AI change what people understand, how they form decisions, and with what economic and societal consequences?**

> **How does AI change how research is conducted, what we can measure, and what we consider credible knowledge?**

## Current Work

My current work studies the `reliability of large language models in research workflows`: how AI systems support, distort, or constrain tasks such as literature synthesis, hypothesis generation, scientific revision, measurement, evaluation, and interpretation.

A central theme is the gap between surface-level fluency and measured behavioral reliability. I am especially interested in whether models preserve constraints, propagate implications, distinguish evidence from plausibility, and remain faithful to the objectives of the research task.
