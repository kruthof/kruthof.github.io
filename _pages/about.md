I am a researcher working at the intersection of `AI`, `information processing`, and `economics`.

My research examines how complex information is interpreted, incorporated into decisions, and translated into economic outcomes, especially in settings where attention is limited, uncertainty is high, and increasingly, `AI systems mediate understanding`.

A central question running through my work is:

> **How does AI change what economic actors understand, how quickly they understand it, and with what consequences?**

At the same time, I study a complementary question:

> **How does AI change how research itself is conducted, what we are able to measure, and what we consider credible knowledge?**

---

## Current Research Focus

My current research focuses on:

- `attention to public information` and the delayed incorporation of complex institutional and macroeconomic text into markets  
- `AI-based measurement and signal extraction` from institutional, macroeconomic, and media text  
- the role of `ambiguity, uncertainty, and interpretive frictions` in shaping when information becomes economically consequential  
- `measurement and validity in AI-mediated environments`, including the credibility, robustness, and time-validity of AI-derived text measures  
- the use of `AI in the research process itself`, including how large language models and related systems transform hypothesis generation, measurement, interpretation, and replication  

---

## Research Perspective

A broader theme in my work is that financial markets provide a powerful empirical setting for studying information processing. Prices can reveal how information is incorporated across channels and under different uncertainty regimes.

I therefore use markets not only as outcomes of interest, but also as observables of `interpretation`, `attention`, and `belief formation`.

At the same time, the increasing use of AI in both markets and research introduces a new layer:  
**we observe not only how humans interpret information, but how interpretations are shaped, filtered, or even generated by AI systems.**

---

## Methodological Interests

I am particularly interested in the methodological foundations of AI in empirical research. This includes work on:

- `look-ahead bias` and the temporal validity of AI-based measures  
- `information leakage` in model training and evaluation  
- the design of AI-based measurement approaches that are more `interpretable`, `auditable`, and `robust`  
- the impact of AI tools on the `research workflow`, including risks of automation bias, overfitting to benchmarks, and loss of transparency  
- strategies to ensure `reproducibility, credibility, and epistemic robustness` in AI-augmented research environments  

Methodologically, I combine `machine learning`, `text-based empirical analysis`, and `economic modeling` to study how technological change affects both information environments and the production of knowledge itself.
