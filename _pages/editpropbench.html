EditPropBench — Human Validation Study

Context: EditPropBench measures whether AI models propagate edits consistently when revising scientific manuscripts. When the author changes one fact (e.g., "230 documents" → "95 documents"), AI models should update every passage whose meaning depends on it — including qualitative descriptions like "large-scale" or "comprehensive" that imply the underlying number.

Your task: For each item, you will see (1) the full original manuscript, (2) the edit instruction the AI was given, and (3) one specific highlighted unit shown in BOTH its original and AI-revised forms. Your job is to judge whether the AI did the right thing for THAT unit.

For each unit you answer up to 4 sequential questions:
  • Q1. Given the edit instruction, should this highlighted text have been changed in the revision?
  • Q2. (if Q1 = yes) Was the highlighted text actually changed in the AI's revision?
  • Q3a. (if Q2 = yes/partial) Is the new wording factually consistent with the new fact value?
  • Q3b. (if Q2 = yes/partial) Is the new wording well expressed (grammatical, natural English)?
Important:
  • Read the edit instruction first to understand what the author wanted
  • Skim the full manuscript for context
  • Focus on the highlighted unit — the original and revised versions are shown side-by-side
  • You judge meaning consistency with the edit, not writing quality or style
  • "Reasonable" means a competent human editor would accept the rewrite as logically consistent — exact word choice doesn't have to match a specific phrasing
Time: ~2 hours for all items. Your progress is auto-saved in your browser. You can also download a save file to resume on a different computer.

Single-step boundary changes (e.g., 5→4 models) are genuinely ambiguous — pick unsure if you're unsure.
Quick reference:
Q1 yes: the unit's claim depends on the value being edited; it needs updating to stay consistent.
Q1 no: the unit doesn't reference the edited fact (or its dependency); leaving it unchanged is correct.
Q2 yes: the AI rewrote the unit. Q2 partial: only some words changed. Q2 no: identical to original.
Q3a (factual): ignore wording quality — does the new text say something compatible with the updated fact value?
Q3b (expression): ignore meaning — does the new text read as natural English (no broken syntax, dangling clauses, awkward inserts)?

Boundary cases: when the new value sits AT or NEAR a band edge (e.g., 5→4 models, 95→105 documents), both Q1=yes and Q1=no are defensible. When in doubt, pick "unsure" — that's a valid answer, not a failure.

The decision is about meaning, not arithmetic. Ask: would a careful reader notice that the original phrasing now feels slightly off given the new value? If yes → Q1=yes. If the phrasing still reads naturally → Q1=no.
