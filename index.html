I am an interdisciplinary researcher working at the intersection of AI, information processing, and economics.

My work examines how complex information is interpreted, incorporated into decisions, and translated into economic outcomes, especially in settings where attention is limited, uncertainty is high, and increasingly, AI systems mediate understanding.

A central question running through my research is:

How does AI change what people understand, how they form judgments, and with what economic and societal consequences?

A second, closely related question concerns the production of knowledge itself:

How does AI change how research is conducted, what we are able to measure, and what we consider credible knowledge?

Taken together, these questions place my work at the intersection of economic decision-making, AI-mediated interpretation, and AI for research workflow.

Research Focus

My current research focuses on:

  • information processing in economic settings, especially how complex institutional and macroeconomic text is incorporated into markets under limited attention and uncertainty
  • AI-based measurement and signal extraction from institutional, macroeconomic, and media text
  • the role of ambiguity, uncertainty, and interpretive frictions in shaping when and how information becomes economically consequential
  • measurement, validity, and credibility in AI-mediated environments, including the robustness and temporal validity of AI-derived text measures
  • the use of AI in the research process itself, including how large language models and related systems affect hypothesis generation, measurement, interpretation, replication, and epistemic quality

Research Perspective

A broader theme in my work is that markets provide a powerful empirical setting for studying information processing. Prices can reveal how information is interpreted and incorporated across channels, institutions, and uncertainty regimes.

I therefore use markets not only as outcomes of interest, but also as observables of interpretation, attention, and belief formation.

At the same time, the growing use of AI in both decision environments and research introduces a further layer of interest: we observe not only how humans interpret information, but also how interpretation is increasingly shaped, filtered, accelerated, or generated by AI systems.

This motivates a broader research agenda on AI mediation: how AI changes the relationship between information, understanding, judgment, and action.

Methodological Interests

I am particularly interested in the methodological foundations of AI in empirical research. This includes work on:

  • look-ahead bias and the temporal validity of AI-based measures
  • information leakage in model training and evaluation
  • the design of AI-based measurement approaches that are more interpretable, auditable, and robust
  • the impact of AI tools on the research workflow, including risks of automation bias, benchmark overfitting, and reduced transparency
  • strategies to ensure reproducibility, credibility, and epistemic robustness in AI-augmented research environments

Methodologically, I combine machine learning, text-based empirical analysis, and economic modeling to study how technological change affects both information environments and the production of knowledge itself.

Broader Direction

My long-term interest is in understanding how AI reshapes judgment, expertise, and knowledge production in high-stakes domains.

While much of my empirical work is situated in finance and economics, the underlying questions extend more broadly to areas such as science, public decision-making, and medicine: wherever complex information must be interpreted under uncertainty, and where AI increasingly acts as an intermediary.

Background

My background spans data science, finance, and research.

Before and alongside my doctoral work, I worked in data science and management consulting, and I also taught as a lecturer at a university of applied sciences in Switzerland. These experiences continue to shape my interest in the intersection of analytical rigor, real-world decision-making, and the broader societal implications of AI.

My academic background includes:

  • MA in Banking and Finance, University of St. Gallen
